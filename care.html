Care guides

Small tanks punish mistakes fast.

I've crashed tanks and I've kept tanks running for over a year without touching them. The difference usually came down to a few basics I'm going to cover here.

Nano basics Cycling Shrimp 101 Choosing fish Planting small Equipment
01

What actually counts as "nano"

Most people call anything under 20–30 liters nano, but the exact number matters less than understanding how small volumes behave. Temperature swings faster. pH drops faster. One missed top-off or an overfed afternoon hits a 10L tank way harder than it would hit a 100L tank.

That's not a reason to avoid them. It's just a reason to be deliberate. The tanks I've kept running the longest are all heavily planted, lightly stocked, and on a routine — not a "fix it when something looks wrong" schedule.

02

Cycling before you stock anything

Cycling grows the bacteria that break down fish waste — ammonia into nitrite, nitrite into nitrate. In a nano tank that colony has almost no buffer. A tank that looks perfectly clear can crash within two days of adding livestock if it isn't cycled. Test for ammonia and nitrite. Clear water doesn't mean safe water.

Plants help — they pull ammonia directly, so a planted tank cycles faster than a bare one. Still, give a new tank several weeks before adding anything sensitive. Crystal shrimp especially. And add livestock gradually so the bacteria can keep up.

03

Shrimp keeping 101

Shrimp are more sensitive than fish to sudden swings and way more sensitive to copper. Copper is lethal to shrimp even in trace amounts — and it shows up in some fertilizers and tap water. Check everything before you add it to a shrimp tank.

Neocaridina — cherries, blue dreams, and similar — are where I'd tell anyone to start. They handle a wider range of water conditions and breed without much fuss. Caridina like crystal reds need soft, acidic, stable water and a tank that's been running for months. Not a beginner project.

04

Choosing nano fish without overstocking

The most common mistake isn't picking the wrong fish — it's adding too many of the right one. Chili rasboras, CPDs, micro rasboras — they all need groups of six to eight minimum to behave normally and show good color. That adds up fast in a small tank.

Pick one schooling species, stock the full group, and leave it there. A 10L tank with a proper school of chili rasboras looks better and runs more stable than one split between four different species.

05

Planting for small spaces

Light and CO2 have to match. Crank up the light without CO2 and you're growing algae, not plants. If you're running low-tech, keep the light moderate and stick with plants that don't need much — anubias, java moss, bucephalandra. Tie them to hardscape and leave them alone.

If you want a carpet — hairgrass, monte carlo — you need medium-high light and pressurized CO2 from day one. These plants don't just grow slowly without CO2. They melt.

06

Equipment essentials

Filter flow is where I see the most beginner mistakes. A filter rated for 40L will turn a 5L shrimp tank into a blender. Get something with adjustable flow, or put a pre-filter sponge on the intake so babies don't get sucked in.

Same with heaters. A 100W heater in a 5L tank will overshoot constantly. Nano heaters in the 15–25W range hold temperature way more precisely. For lights, a small clip-on or bar light is usually enough — a full-size fixture is almost always overkill.
